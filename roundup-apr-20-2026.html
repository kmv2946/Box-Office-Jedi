‘Mario’ Holds, ‘Hail Mary’ Enters Rare Territory, ‘Lee’ Crones

By Box Office Jedi

April 20, 2026

We came in close on The Super Mario Galaxy Movie again — over by about $1 million on our forecast. The trajectory still points to a final in the neighborhood of what Minecraft managed last year, and over $100 million shy of the first Mario. Not bad for a second movie in a billion-dollar franchise, but not exactly a victory lap either.

▶ Mario Franchise Showdown >>

There’s not much more to dig into on Mario itself, but here’s a fun use for the new toggle we just rolled out that lets you adjust any chart on the site for ticket-price inflation: Mario Galaxy’s current total is roughly in line with Dr. Dolittle (1998), once you bring Dolittle up to 2026’s average ticket price. Everything used to be a hit. Or nothing is now a hit. Depends on how you squint.

The most interesting thing that happened this weekend — and it doesn’t happen often — is that Project Hail Mary joined the top ten highest-grossing fifth weekends in domestic box office history. With $20.5 million in its fifth frame, it slots in tied with The Avengers, and just a hair behind Barbie.

A quick asterisk: we’re excluding Paranormal Activity and two Clint Eastwood films — American Sniper and Gran Torino — even though most charts will show them ranking here. Their first weekends were in limited release, and counting those as the first few weekends skews the comparison. We want wide-release versus wide-release.

If you’re still hovering above $20 million on your fifth weekend, you’ve done one of two things. You either opened so enormously that it took five full weeks to come down to that level — think Star Wars: The Force Awakens, Black Panther, The Avengers — or you’re simply so well-liked that you’ve been camped there the whole time. That second group is much smaller. It’s essentially Titanic and The Sixth Sense, two of the leggiest blockbusters in modern history. The latter feat is, in our opinion, vastly more impressive. Posting $20 million on your fifth weekend is easy if you opened to $200 million. Posting it when you opened to $26.7 million is something else entirely.

The chart above tells the story. Project Hail Mary splits the difference between the two phenomena. It opened reasonably — higher than The Sixth Sense, nowhere near The Avengers — and then dropped so slowly that it’s now hanging around on the same weekend shelf as films that opened two or three times bigger. Worth noting: Titanic and the two Avatars are still the only three films ever to gross more than $30 million on a fifth weekend. Nothing from the superhero era has come close. Maybe next week we’ll check in on sixth weekends.
