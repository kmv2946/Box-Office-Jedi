‘Michael’ to Dethrone ‘Mario’ as Box Office King

by Box Office Jedi

Thursday, April 23, 2026

Angels are back this weekend and it feels more than divine. It’s been exactly 30 years since John Travolta’s Michael was released in cinemas, going on to make $95.3 million at the domestic box office—$240 million adjusted to 2026, more than Captain America: Brave New World made last year.

There’s a cyclical thing in the air, with the reboot (sequel? requel?) of Michael arriving 30 years later, and next weekend The Devil Wears Prada 2 landing 20 years after its predecessor. With Michael getting a big IMAX push, it’s being positioned as a major comeback for the IP—and, well, for John Travolta, too. He hasn’t had a real onscreen breakout since Wild Hogs in 2007.

The bigger question than whether Travolta can still draw a crowd is whether religious-adjacent blockbusters still resonate. Things were different in the 90s, when Christianity was just a given, something the public breathed like air, and movies about angels or some strain of divinity were all the rage: City of Angels, The Preacher’s Wife, Angels in the Outfield, Dear God, Holy Man—where Eddie Murphy turns the Home Shopping Network into a religious experience. Even the summer blockbuster Deep Impact features a monologue from Morgan Freeman, playing the president of the United States, who begins his address to the nation with, “I believe in God…”

EDIT: Michael is an American biographical film about Michael Jackson, directed by Antoine Fuqua and starring Jaafar Jackson, Michael’s real-life nephew.

Its comps are all over the place. The most recent is Elvis, which opened to $31.2 million in 2022 but multiplied beautifully to a $151 million domestic total. But Elvis wasn’t positioned as an IMAX release (Top Gun: Maverick still held onto most of those screens). A closer comp might be Bohemian Rhapsody, which opened to $51 million and totaled $216.7 million domestically—and whose international tripled its domestic gross, nearly earning it a rare $1 billion worldwide total. We expect Michael to dominate overseas in the same way. How well it’ll do here is still kind of blurry. Projections are calling for $65–85 million this weekend, but we’re sensing a little lower, for whatever reason. Straight Outta Compton holds the record for biggest opening for a musical biopic with $60.2 million in 2015, so that’s on the line.

Expect Lee Cronin’s The Mummy to fall hard in its second weekend. Super Mario Galaxy continues to trail its predecessor by more and more, despite holding a little better than estimates suggested last weekend, and Project Hail Mary loses its premium format screens to Michael, so expect a bigger dip than usual—though that film feels like it’s operating on its own intergalactic plane, and it’ll have another soft landing.
