2026 Summer Preview

Signs, signals, and our rankings for the summer of ’26.

by Box Office Jedi

April 22, 2026

There’s something about this summer that is concerning. Not in a bad way. But somewhere in this lineup, it feels like there’s a warning. They subconsciously point to a beginning or an end or both.

Last summer was narrative-less. Weapons, F1, Jurassic World, Lilo and Stitch. If anything, it was Warner Brothers’ last hurrah.

But with May approaching, we find ourselves with a sense that this could be our last hurrah — though not quite in the way that phrase usually lands. It’s like slowly walking out to the edge of a diving board at night. The water is dark, reflecting the stars in the clear sky. You could splash, or you could fall infinitely into space.

A few things that feel… like something —

Scary Movie’s return. This feels like the tentpole in Paramount’s broader end-of-wokeness campaign. However real that is, the Wayans’ return to the franchise feels like some kind of stake coming to firmly impale us. The first Scary Movie was released in 2000, and was a real severing force between the ’90s and the decade to come.

Disclosure Day. In a way, this feels like Spielberg’s last film. It feels like he’s recognized how bad all the movies he’s made since Lincoln have been, and he’s trying to reconcile that while he’s still got the chance. There are shots in the trailer that reference A.I. and Minority Report and War of the Worlds and, of course, Close Encounters. But the thing that feels most timely about the project is how its release coincides with the White House purchasing the domains for alien.gov and aliens.gov.

Spider-Man. This also feels like one last stand for Marvel, and somehow a reminder of how fragmented the pandemic made reality. Some movies feel like they’ve moved on. Other movies feel like they never made it past 2019. And a few feel perpetually stuck in that 2020–2021 chapter and don’t know how to escape, like they got lost in one of Doctor Strange’s dimensions. Spider-Man: Brand New Day’s title strikes us as ironic. It’s a subtle attempt to reboot the Marvel franchise (at some point everyone stopped using the term “universe”) before the new Avengers movie comes out this December. But what it actually feels like is a smoke signal from 2021, when the last Spider-Man came out, asking us to come back and save it. Sounds like an Avengers movie.

Toy Story 5 feels incredibly sad, too. It’s the most nihilistic Pixar movie since Wall-E, where the studio finally grapples with the fact that toys are no longer relevant, as we once knew them. Another smoke signal. Wonder if there’ll be any Lightyear jokes.

The Odyssey, while hyped as hell, doesn’t really feel like part of this arrangement of signs yet to be deciphered. That’s because Christopher Nolan feels apart from anything going on in the film industry — not necessarily in a spearheading way, just… apart. If Hollywood were to evaporate at the snap of a finger, or the world were to end this summer, Christopher Nolan would still be off in some remote land making his next movie. The money will come from somewhere.

* * *

Here’s how we predict everything will play out.

1. Spider-Man: Brand New Day July 31

While this already feels like a disappointment, Spider-Man remains the most reliable IP in Hollywood. It will be the highest-grossing movie of the summer by default, but with a guaranteed decline from Brand New Day’s 2021 predecessor, which benefited from the holiday season and the pandemic drought.

Opening Weekend: $219 million
Domestic Total: $640 million
2. Toy Story 5 June 19

Inside Out 2 was such a success because it was the first sequel to a post-millennial Pixar film, and Toy Story 5 seems to be riding on that with a hook every generation can rally behind: the exhaustion of screens. Despite the nothingness that was Lightyear, we predict this installment will play pretty close to Toy Story 4, if not better. It just feels like the right time — all we can really go off of for the box office lately.

Opening Weekend: $132 million
Domestic Total: $454 million
3. The Odyssey July 17

This feels like a bit of a Trojan horse, giving us pause on ranking it this high. By Trojan horse, we just mean the industry is hauling it in through its gates, ogling it like the second coming of Oppenheimer, and then they open it up and Interstellar is sitting inside. But we’re going to play the Trojans and assume all is good. At the end of the day, it feels like an extended trailer for Dune 3.

We’ll note that the opening weekend will have a strict ceiling, in the way Avatar: The Way of Water did. There are only so many IMAX showtimes, and no one is going to see The Odyssey any other way. They’ll wait until weekend two or three to see it in its intended format, which means a slightly lower opening and slightly better legs than usual.

Opening Weekend: $115 million
Domestic Total: $322 million
4. The Devil Wears Prada 2 May 1

How big will this be? Barbie feels like the ceiling, with $162 million. But we expect it to play closer to Wicked ($112.5 million), with a lot of room to break out. This baby has really marinated.

Opening Weekend: $121 million
Domestic Total: $306 million
5. Moana July 10

They could’ve waited another year or two. This is just gonna confuse kids. Didn’t they just see this a year ago? Why is it back in theaters? Why do they look like that? And Moana, as sunny as the franchise is, simply is not a summer franchise. Moana is for Thanksgiving. This will do fine, but it could’ve done better. Plus competition with Minions.

Opening Weekend: $92 million
Domestic Total: $300 million
6. Minions & Monsters July 1 (Weds)

This franchise is relatively consistent, and even after six installments shows no sign of dying, like the Minions themselves. That said, the totals for the six movies have followed a pattern of increase–decrease–increase–decrease (in order: $251m, $368m, $336m, $264m, $370m, $361m). Yes, they’ve steadied over the years, but we are in a decrease year this installment, and despite the addition of monsters, we’re sticking with the pattern. Dinosaurs didn’t really add much to Ice Age, after all.

Opening Weekend: $68 million (3-day) / $115 million (5-day)
Domestic Total: $276 million
7. The Mandalorian & Grogu May 22

It’s insane that The Rise of Skywalker came out in 2019. It feels like a far more distant memory. If others feel that way, this could turn out well. It could also completely tank, but our instincts are telling us the ill will or ambivalence toward this franchise has worn off a little, and the degree to which this movie seems to not take itself seriously will probably do it a favor. But that also gives it a pretty low ceiling. We’re giving it the benefit of the doubt so LucasFilm and Disney don’t sue us over our name.

Opening Weekend: $96 million
Domestic Total: $238 million
8. Scary Movie June 5

There’s an interesting parallel happening with this one. The first Scream trilogy (Scream 1–3) was released between 1996 and 2000. Scream 3 hit a franchise-high opening weekend with $34.7 million. Scary Movie opened a few months later and outgrossed Scream 3 with a $42.3 million opening weekend.

This year, the same pattern has played out. The new Scream trilogy (Scream 5, 6, 7) was released from 2022–2026, with the latest in the franchise scoring another franchise high with $63.6 million. And again, Scary Movie will come out a few months later. It’s like the Scream reboots were here solely to lead up to the new Scary Movie.

And we expect the box office to play out accordingly. Scary Movie (2000) opened 22% higher than Scream 3, so we’re predicting the same jump from Scream 7.

Opening Weekend: $77.5 million
Domestic Total: $171.5 million
9. Disclosure Day June 12

This could play out terribly, but we suspect it will enter our consciousness as a return of Spielberg — someone who once played for the masses — and now, with rumored alien announcements from the government, it feels like people will be paying attention. With major goodwill from Project Hail Mary and Artemis II, we predict space and its many mysteries will be a welcome topic this summer. The marketing campaign just needs to sing.

Opening Weekend: $58 million
Domestic Total: $155 million
10. Mortal Kombat II May 15

The counter-programming to The Devil Wears Prada 2. The boys will be out in full force, having had their summer inauguration weekend stolen from them.

Opening Weekend: $54 million
Domestic Total: $107 million
* * *

Look out for possible breakouts.

The End of Oak Street — could be a late summer surprise. Jackass: Best and Last — it felt like Jackass Forever could’ve made $400 million, but it capped out at $57m. This also feels like it could do either/or. Billie Eilish: Hit Me Hard and Soft — The Tour Live 3D — the real wildcard. Masters of the Universe — remember when Pirates of the Caribbean felt like a sure bomb in 2003, coming off the swashbuckling disaster Cutthroat Island and when Disney was in a slump. You never know. Ice Cream Man — this could hit just right. Backrooms — probably not.

And no, we didn’t forget to rank Supergirl.
