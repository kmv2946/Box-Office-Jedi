‘Mario’ Repeats, ‘Hail Mary’ Enters Rare Company

By Box Office Jedi

April 13, 2026

Super Mario Galaxy Movie repeated at number one with $68.1 million, a 47% drop from its opening weekend. We had it at $67.3 million in our forecast. Under a million off. We’ll take it.

After 12 days, Galaxy stands at $308 million domestic and is now the year’s highest-grossing film, having just overtaken Project Hail Mary. Where does it land? Somewhere between the first Mario and Minecraft, or so the trajectory suggests.

See how they’re tracking side-by-side:

▶ Mario Franchise Showdown >>

Project Hail Mary dropped just 23% in its fourth weekend, adding $24.5 million for a domestic total of $256.6 million. It immediately brings a lot of comps to mind. That’s not to say there are many — it’s just such a specific space to be, and you don’t forget numbers like that easily.

Most recently, Sinners had an almost identical fourth weekend, taking in $23 million on the way to a $275+ million domestic finish. It’s also close to Gravity’s fourth weekend (like Sinners, it finished around $275 million), as well as the fourth weekend of Lord and Miller’s prior film, The Lego Movie, which did $21 million on a 33% drop, finishing at $257 million. Oppenheimer made $18.8 million in its fourth weekend, a 35% drop, putting it at roughly $265 million domestic at that point — about $10 million ahead of where Hail Mary sits now. But Hail Mary is holding tighter, so we’re predicting it’ll finish higher. Oppenheimer did benefit from a small Oscar-season re-expansion in early 2024, but who’s to say Hail Mary doesn’t get the same? A $330+ million domestic finish looks right, which would put it just above Oppenheimer’s final $330 million.

▶ Post-Pandemic Blockbuster “Originals” Showdown — Oppenheimer, Sinners, PHM >>

The Drama dropped just 21% in its second weekend, from $14.3 million to $8.6 million, bringing its domestic total to $30.8 million after 10 days. For context: Materialists, the last A24 “romance” of this scale, dropped 49% in its second weekend. The Drama is playing more like Challengers than like Materialists, and Challengers opened to $15 million, essentially the same place. Materialists finished with around $35 million domestic total. The Drama should match that by the end of this week. From there, it’s a question of how Long Legs it has. It’s on track for $55 million domestic, which would make it one of the highest-grossing A24 wide releases ever.

▶ A24 Wide Release Showdown — Everything Everywhere, Marty Supreme, The Drama, Civil War, Uncut Gems >>

Hoppers added $4.1 million in its sixth weekend, crossing $157 million domestic. That puts it past Elemental’s $154.4 million domestic total, though Elemental, in one of the great overperformances in recent Pixar history, went on to $496 million globally. Hoppers is not doing that. Its worldwide total currently sits around $354 million. The film ran out of steam faster than expected — Mario Galaxy arrived a weekend too soon — but it has already secured the title of highest-grossing domestic original animated release since Pixar’s Coco in 2017.

And in what is becoming predictably unpredictable, a handful of movies snuck into the top ten that no one knew was on the guest list. This happens almost every weekend now. A concert movie that has one showtime charging $200 per ticket. The penultimate episode of a reality show no one has heard of except for a very niche but rabid audience. Something that has 20 words in the title. This week’s additions were Faces of Death, Exit 8, and BTS World Tour ‘Arirang’ in Goyang: Live Viewing.

Exit 8 was on our forecast. Neon acquired the North American rights last August after the film had already grossed $27 million in Japan, where it was the highest-grossing live-action film of 2025. It’s adapted from a 2023 indie walking simulator by Kotake Create, in which a man loops endlessly through a Tokyo subway corridor, trying to spot anomalies and reach the exit. It premiered at TIFF and screened at Midnight Madness. It cost under $4 million to make. It has a 95% on Rotten Tomatoes, which technically makes it the highest-rated video game adaptation of all time. Neon sent a guy in a Walking Man costume to wander New York City subway stations the Tuesday before release. People who approached him and said a passphrase got a prize (we hear it was a $50 Uber gift card). It took $1.4 million from 495 theaters, which is a strong per-screen average, and it’s a natural conversation partner for A24’s upcoming Backrooms, another liminal horror with a micro-budget premise.
